How to Manage Your Investments with a Google Sheets Rental Property Tracking Spreadsheet

Running a rental property business can be incredibly lucrative, but it also comes with a mountain of bookkeeping.

Between tracking monthly rent payments, monitoring maintenance expenses, managing security deposits, and preparing for tax season, landlords have to handle an overwhelming amount of financial data.

Many new real estate investors start by using complex, expensive property management software. However, these platforms often come with steep learning curves and high monthly subscription costs that eat directly into your rental yields.

For most independent landlords, the ultimate solution is a custom google sheets rental property tracking spreadsheet.

In this guide, we will show you how to structure your real estate bookkeeping in Google Sheets to maximize your deductions and keep your investments profitable.

Why Google Sheets is Perfect for Landlords

Google Sheets is the preferred tool for thousands of independent landlords worldwide for several key reasons:

  • Real-Time Cloud Access: Access your financial logs from your smartphone while visiting properties, or share the spreadsheet with your co-owners or accountant in real time.
  • Zero Monthly Cost: Unlike premium software that charges per-unit fees, Google Sheets is completely free to use.
  • Powerful Automation: Use built-in formulas to automatically calculate net operating income (NOI), cash-on-cash return, and capitalization rates across your entire portfolio.

The Essential Columns Every Rental Tracker Needs

To keep your real estate bookkeeping tax-ready, your spreadsheet should be organized into three core tabs:

Tab 1: Property Portfolio Overview

A master list of your properties, including the purchase price, mortgage details, property tax rates, and current occupancy status.

Tab 2: Income Ledger

A simple, chronological log of all rental income received. Make sure to include columns for:

  • Date Received
  • Property Address / Unit Number
  • Tenant Name
  • Payment Method (Stripe, ACH, Check)
  • Amount Paid

Tab 3: Expense Ledger

Log every penny you spend on property maintenance, insurance, marketing, and management. Categorize each expense according to Schedule E (Form 1040) tax categories (e.g., repairs, cleaning, utilities, insurance) to save hours of stress during tax season.
